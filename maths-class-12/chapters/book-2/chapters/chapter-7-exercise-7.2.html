Question 1

Given:

$$ \int\frac{2x}{1+x^2}\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ u=1+x^2. $$

Then,

$$ \frac{du}{dx}=2x $$

or

$$ du=2x\,dx. $$

Substituting,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\frac{2x}{1+x^2}\,dx &=\int\frac{du}{u}\\ &=\log|u|+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\frac{2x}{1+x^2}\,dx = \log(1+x^2)+C } $$

Question 2

Given:

$$ \int\frac{(\log x)^2}{x}\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ u=\log x. $$

Then,

$$ du=\frac1x\,dx. $$

Substituting,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\frac{(\log x)^2}{x}\,dx &=\int u^2\,du\\ &=\frac{u^3}{3}+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\frac{(\log x)^2}{x}\,dx = \frac{(\log x)^3}{3}+C } $$

Question 3

Given:

$$ \int\frac1{x+x\log x}\,dx $$

Solution:

Rewrite the denominator.

$$ x+x\log x=x(1+\log x). $$

Hence,

$$ \int\frac1{x(1+\log x)}\,dx. $$

Let

$$ u=1+\log x. $$

Then,

$$ du=\frac1x\,dx. $$

Therefore,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\frac1{x(1+\log x)}\,dx &=\int\frac{du}{u}\\ &=\log|u|+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\frac1{x+x\log x}\,dx = \log|1+\log x|+C } $$

$$ du=-\sin x\,dx. $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\sin x\,\sin(\cos x)\,dx = \cos(\cos x)+C } $$

Question 5

Given:

$$ \int\sin(ax+b)\cos(ax+b)\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ u=\sin(ax+b). $$

Then,

$$ du=a\cos(ax+b)\,dx. $$

or

$$ \cos(ax+b)\,dx=\frac{du}{a}. $$

Substituting,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\sin(ax+b)\cos(ax+b)\,dx &=\frac1a\int u\,du\\ &=\frac{u^2}{2a}+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\sin(ax+b)\cos(ax+b)\,dx = \frac{\sin^2(ax+b)}{2a}+C } $$

Question 6

Given:

$$ \int\sqrt{ax+b}\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ u=ax+b. $$

Then,

$$ du=a\,dx $$

or

$$ dx=\frac{du}{a}. $$

Therefore,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\sqrt{ax+b}\,dx &=\frac1a\int u^{1/2}\,du\\ &=\frac1a\cdot\frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2}+C\\ &=\frac{2}{3a}u^{3/2}+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\sqrt{ax+b}\,dx = \frac{2}{3a}(ax+b)^{3/2}+C } $$

Question 7

Given:

$$ \int x\sqrt{x+2}\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ u=x+2. $$

Then,

$$ x=u-2, \qquad du=dx. $$

Substituting,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int x\sqrt{x+2}\,dx &=\int(u-2)u^{1/2}\,du\\ &=\int\left(u^{3/2}-2u^{1/2}\right)du. \end{aligned} $$

Integrating,

$$ \begin{aligned} &=\frac{2}{5}u^{5/2}-\frac{4}{3}u^{3/2}+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int x\sqrt{x+2}\,dx = \frac25(x+2)^{5/2} - \frac43(x+2)^{3/2} +C } $$

Replacing \(u\),

Question 10

Given:

$$ \int\frac1{x-\sqrt{x}}\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ \sqrt{x}=t. $$

Then,

$$ x=t^2, \qquad dx=2t\,dt. $$

Substituting,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\frac1{x-\sqrt{x}}\,dx &=\int\frac{2t}{t^2-t}\,dt\\ &=\int\frac{2}{t-1}\,dt. \end{aligned} $$

Therefore,

$$ \begin{aligned} &=2\log|t-1|+C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(t=\sqrt{x}\),

$$ \boxed{ \int\frac1{x-\sqrt{x}}\,dx = 2\log|\sqrt{x}-1|+C } $$

Question 19

Given:

$$ \int\frac{e^{2x}-1}{e^{2x}+1}\,dx $$

Solution:

Let

$$ u=e^{2x}+1. $$

Then,

$$ du=2e^{2x}\,dx. $$

Rewrite the numerator as

$$ e^{2x}-1=(e^{2x}+1)-2=u-2. $$

Also,

$$ e^{2x}=\frac{u-1}{}, $$

which leads to unnecessary algebra. A simpler approach is to write

$$ \frac{e^{2x}-1}{e^{2x}+1} = 1-\frac{2}{e^{2x}+1}. $$

Hence,

$$ \int\frac{e^{2x}-1}{e^{2x}+1}\,dx = x-\int\frac{2}{e^{2x}+1}\,dx. $$

Now let

$$ t=e^{2x}. $$

Then

$$ dt=2e^{2x}\,dx, \qquad dx=\frac{dt}{2t}. $$

After substitution,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\frac{2}{e^{2x}+1}\,dx &=\int\frac{dt}{t(t+1)}\\ &=\int\left(\frac1t-\frac1{t+1}\right)dt\\ &=\log t-\log(t+1). \end{aligned} $$

Therefore,

$$ \boxed{ \int\frac{e^{2x}-1}{e^{2x}+1}\,dx = -x+\log(e^{2x}+1)+C } $$

Question 21

Given:

$$ \int \tan^2(2x-3)\,dx $$

Solution:

Using the identity

$$ \tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta-1, $$

we get

$$ \int \tan^2(2x-3)\,dx = \int\left[\sec^2(2x-3)-1\right]dx. $$

Therefore,

$$ = \int\sec^2(2x-3)\,dx-\int dx. $$

Let

$$ u=2x-3. $$

Then

$$ du=2\,dx \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad dx=\frac{du}{2}. $$

Hence,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int\sec^2(2x-3)\,dx &=\frac12\int\sec^2u\,du\\ &=\frac12\tan u. \end{aligned} $$

Substituting back,

$$ \boxed{ \int \tan^2(2x-3)\,dx = \frac12\tan(2x-3)-x+C } $$

Question 32

Given:

$$ \int \frac1{1+\cot x} \,dx $$

Solution:

Multiply the numerator and denominator by \(\sin x\).

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac1{1+\cot x} &= \frac{\sin x}{\sin x+\cos x}. \end{aligned} $$

Rewrite the numerator.

$$ \sin x = \frac12\left[(\sin x+\cos x)+(\sin x-\cos x)\right]. $$

Hence,

$$ \begin{aligned} I &= \frac12 \int \left[ 1+ \frac{\sin x-\cos x} {\sin x+\cos x} \right] dx. \end{aligned} $$

Now let

$$ u=\sin x+\cos x. $$

Then

$$ du=(\cos x-\sin x)\,dx. $$

Therefore,

$$ (\sin x-\cos x)\,dx=-du. $$

Hence,

$$ \begin{aligned} I &= \frac12 \left[ x-\int\frac{du}{u} \right] +C\\ &= \frac12 \left[ x-\log|u| \right] +C. \end{aligned} $$

Replacing \(u\),

$$ \boxed{ \int \frac1{1+\cot x} \,dx = \frac12 \left( x-\log|\sin x+\cos x| \right) +C } $$

Using the identity

$$ \frac1{\sin^2x\cos^2x} = \sec^2x+\cosec^2x, $$

because

$$ \begin{aligned} \sec^2x+\cosec^2x &= \frac1{\cos^2x} + \frac1{\sin^2x}\\ &= \frac{\sin^2x+\cos^2x} {\sin^2x\cos^2x}\\ &= \frac1{\sin^2x\cos^2x}. \end{aligned} $$

Therefore,

$$ \begin{aligned} \int \frac{dx} {\sin^2x\cos^2x} &= \int\sec^2x\,dx + \int\cosec^2x\,dx. \end{aligned} $$

Using the standard integrals,

$$ \int\sec^2x\,dx=\tan x, $$

and

$$ \int\cosec^2x\,dx=-\cot x. $$

Hence,

$$ \boxed{ \int \frac{dx} {\sin^2x\cos^2x} = \tan x-\cot x+C } $$
