CBSEChapter-4 Questions and activities

Questions and Activities

1. Do you think life became easier or more challenging after humans started farming? Give two reasons for your answer.

Answer:

Life became easier in many ways after humans started farming, although it also brought some new challenges.

Two reasons are:

  1. Regular Food Supply: Farming provided a steady supply of food, reducing the uncertainty of hunting and gathering. People could grow crops and store surplus food for future use.
  2. Permanent Settlements: Agriculture encouraged people to settle in one place. This led to the development of villages, better houses, domestication of animals, and the growth of trade and civilization.

However, farming also required hard work, protection of crops, and dependence on weather conditions.

2. The environment offers human societies both opportunities as well as challenges. Explain with reference to early farming communities and river-valley civilisations.

Answer:

The environment played an important role in the development of early farming communities and river-valley civilizations. It provided many opportunities, but it also created several challenges.

Opportunities:

  • Fertile soil near rivers helped in successful farming.
  • Availability of water made irrigation and agriculture possible.
  • Rivers provided fish, transportation, and trade routes.
  • Natural resources such as clay, wood, and stones supported house construction and tool making.

Challenges:

  • Floods sometimes destroyed crops and settlements.
  • Droughts caused water shortages and crop failures.
  • Wild animals threatened people and livestock.
  • Changes in climate affected agricultural production.

Thus, early humans learned to adapt to their environment by developing farming techniques, irrigation systems, and permanent settlements.

3. Why do historians divide early human history into different ages such as Stone Age, Bronze Age, and Iron Age? What does this classification tell us about human progress?

Answer:

Historians divide early human history into the Stone Age, Bronze Age, and Iron Age based on the materials used for making tools and weapons. Each age represents a stage in the development of human technology and society.

This classification shows that human beings gradually improved their skills and knowledge. They moved from using simple stone tools to stronger bronze tools and finally to durable iron tools. These technological advancements led to better farming, improved craftsmanship, increased trade, stronger settlements, and the growth of civilizations.

Therefore, this classification reflects the continuous progress of human life from simple hunting communities to organized and advanced societies.

4. Imagine you are a Neolithic farmer. Describe one day of your life. What challenges would you face that a hunter-gatherer would not?

Answer:

I wake up early in the morning and go to my fields to check my crops. I water the plants, remove weeds, and protect them from birds and wild animals. I also take care of my domesticated animals by feeding and watering them. During the day, I repair farming tools, collect firewood, and help my family store grains for future use. In the evening, I return home and prepare for the next day's work.

Challenges I would face:

  • I would have to protect my crops from pests, birds, and wild animals.
  • I would depend on rainfall and favourable weather for a good harvest.
  • I would need to work hard throughout the year to grow food.
  • I would have to store grains safely to prevent spoilage and theft.
  • I could not move freely from one place to another because I had permanent fields and a settlement.

Unlike hunter-gatherers, my life would be more settled, but it would also involve greater responsibility and continuous hard work.

5. Imagine that the Harappan script gets deciphered tomorrow. What new types of information do you think historians might learn?

Answer:

If the Harappan script were deciphered, historians would gain valuable information about the Harappan Civilization. It would help answer many questions that still remain a mystery.

Historians might learn about:

  • The language spoken by the Harappan people.
  • The names of their rulers, important people, and cities.
  • Their system of government and administration.
  • Their religious beliefs, customs, and festivals.
  • Their trade relations with other civilizations.
  • Their laws, occupations, and daily life.
  • The reasons behind the decline of the Harappan Civilization.

Deciphering the Harappan script would greatly improve our understanding of one of the world's earliest civilizations.

7. "Bronze Age civilisations developed independently but shared common features." Examine this statement with reference to the civilisations given in the chapter.

Answer:

The Bronze Age civilizations, such as the Harappan Civilization, Mesopotamian Civilization, Egyptian Civilization, and Chinese Civilization, developed independently in different parts of the world. However, they shared many common characteristics.

Common Features:

  • Most civilizations developed near river valleys.
  • Agriculture was the main occupation.
  • Bronze tools and weapons were widely used.
  • Well-planned settlements and permanent houses were built.
  • Trade and commerce flourished.
  • Writing systems developed for record-keeping.
  • People followed organized governments and social structures.
  • Religion played an important role in society.

Although these civilizations evolved independently, similar environmental conditions and human needs led to the development of many common features.

8. Although rivers provided many benefits, they also created challenges for early societies. Discuss both the advantages and disadvantages of settling near rivers.

Answer:

Advantages:

  • Fertile soil suitable for agriculture.
  • Availability of water for drinking and irrigation.
  • Easy transportation and trade.
  • Availability of fish and other natural resources.
  • Growth of towns and civilizations.

Disadvantages:

  • Frequent floods damaged crops and settlements.
  • Waterborne diseases spread easily.
  • Riverbanks were prone to erosion.
  • Changes in river courses sometimes destroyed settlements.
  • Dependence on rivers made people vulnerable during droughts.

Thus, rivers supported the growth of civilizations but also exposed people to natural hazards.

9. With the help of your teacher, find out more about the Code of Hammurabi. Why was it important? Do you think it was fair to all sections of society? Give reasons for your answer.

Answer:

The Code of Hammurabi was one of the earliest written law codes, created by King Hammurabi of Babylon around 1754 BCE. It contained nearly 282 laws dealing with crime, trade, family matters, and property.

Importance:

  • It established clear rules and punishments.
  • It helped maintain law and order.
  • It protected property rights and regulated trade.
  • It ensured that justice was based on written laws rather than personal opinions.

Was it fair to all sections of society?

The Code of Hammurabi was not completely fair because punishments often depended on a person's social status. Wealthy people, commoners, and slaves were treated differently under the law. Therefore, although it was an important step towards organized justice, it did not provide equal rights to everyone.

10. If you had to choose one major innovation from early civilizations that changed the world permanently, what would it be and why?

Answer:

I would choose the invention of writing as the most important innovation of early civilizations.

Reasons:

  • It enabled people to record information and preserve knowledge.
  • It helped governments maintain laws and administrative records.
  • It promoted trade through written accounts.
  • It allowed literature, history, and scientific knowledge to be passed on to future generations.
  • It laid the foundation for modern education and communication.

Without writing, the growth of civilizations and the advancement of human knowledge would have been much slower.

11. Compare the social hierarchy and daily life of people in the Egyptian civilization with those in Mesopotamia or China. What similarities and differences do you notice?

Answer:

Basis Egyptian Civilization Mesopotamian Civilization
Social Hierarchy Pharaoh at the top, followed by priests, officials, soldiers, artisans, farmers, and slaves. King at the top, followed by priests, nobles, merchants, artisans, farmers, and slaves.
Main Occupation Agriculture along the Nile River. Agriculture along the Tigris and Euphrates Rivers.
Religion Belief in many gods and life after death. Belief in many gods connected with nature.
Writing Hieroglyphic script. Cuneiform script.
Buildings Pyramids and temples. Ziggurats and temples.

Similarities:

  • Both civilizations had a well-organized social hierarchy.
  • Agriculture was the main occupation.
  • Both believed in many gods.
  • Both developed writing systems and advanced architecture.

Differences:

  • Egypt was centred around the Nile River, while Mesopotamia developed between the Tigris and Euphrates Rivers.
  • The Egyptians built pyramids, whereas the Mesopotamians built ziggurats.
  • Egyptians used hieroglyphics, while Mesopotamians used cuneiform writing.
